Heart of Texas Lab Rescue

Challenged to create a Responsive Web Design for a Non-Profit Website in 3 weeks.

Duration:

3 Weeks

Role and Team Size:

Role: UI/UX Designer, Team Size: 3

Teammates: Lillian Graye, Emily Monagle

Tools Used:

Miro, Figma, Excel, InVsion, Trello

Key Skills

Responsive Web Design, Interaction Design, User Interface Design, Stakeholder Interviews, UX Writing, Competitor Analysis

Overview

Problem:

Users struggle to find information that helps them feel confident in adopting a dog from Heart of Texas Lab Rescue.

Solution:

We will create a Responsive Web Design that will be easier to navigate and locate important information for adopting dogs so we can help people find dogs that fit their family needs so that more dogs can get adopted.

Target:

Our target users, are people looking for purebred labs and are looking to save money adopting a dog from a shelter rather than getting one from a breeder.

Home Screens

This project features Responsive Web Design for Desktop, Tablet and Mobile Devices. We chose to focus on Redesigning the Adoption Process since that is the main mission of the HOT Lab Rescue.

Usability Testing

We conducted a set of usability tests in the beginning to see what problems users faced when looking into the adoption process on the current site.

Stakeholder Interviews

We wanted to talk to someone who worked at the HOT Lab Recue to learn more about what they wanted their company was about as well as what aspects of their design they would like to keep in the redesign. We could not reach a person who worked there so instead our professor acted as our stakeholder. Some things mentioned where the importance of volunteers, donations and the color red.

Pain Points

Some key problems people noticed on the site: dog profile pictures were distorted and their captions were disorganized in terms of information they provided for the dogs, the donation button was hard to find and it was unclear what the money was going to, and lastly the adoption process page was too wordy.

Goals

Goals we had moving forward were to fix the key problems that users faced when looking into the adoption process as well as take our stakeholder's information into account to ensure that we were representing the Non-Profit the right way.

Definition and Ideation

User Persona

Our User Persona is actually a couple who is looking to adopt a dog as an anniversary gift to each other. Some goals they have are adopting from the shelter and finding a lab that will get along with the cat they already have. Some pain points they currently face are finding purebred labs from shelters in their area and having a hard time finding a dog compatible with their cat.

Competitor Analyisis

Low Fidelity Wireframing

The way we designed the general layout for the site was by first having all three of us design our own home pages. We wanted to see how everyone was thinking of redesigning the site individually first so everyone had the chance to share their vision. After we all shared we decided to combine the best parts of all three designs into one cohesive home screen layout. Some Features we wanted to include included a large hero image, a double row navigation bar and Donation and Volunteer banners.

UI Style Tile

We then designed what our buttons were going to look like as well as what the dog profiles were going to look like. We wanted to find a color palette that worked well with the color red and decided on the colors above. We also chose the hero image, the icons and the logo we wanted to use. We decided on two different fonts as well. Open Sans for body text and Rubik for headings. It was also important for us to communicate the mission of the site and site this Non-Profit is all about dogs we wanted to put the paw print margin design from the old site into the new one to reiterate the focus on the dogs through visual web components.

Mid-Fidelity Prototypes

For this section of the prototyping we each worked on our own. Emily tackled the desktop prototype, Lillian worked on the tablet prototype and I did the mobile prototype. We felt that was the best move for getting the prototypes done in quick manner.

Mobile, Tablet, and Desktop Prototypes

The reason these all look different is because we decided initially to let everyone pick and choose how the colors from the style guide would be used in the designs as well as we each had different thoughts about how to layout the information for the hero images. I decided not to put the mission statement in the hero image at all because the text would have been too crammed on the desktop version.

User Testing

After we designed the layouts we each tested them on our own. It was interesting that we got a lot of similar feedback across the platforms. Some of the things people pointed out were that the mission statement in the desktop version was hard to read, across platforms people wanted to know if the dogs were compatible with kids or other animals, people thought the rubik font was hard to read as body text, and in the mobile prototype people wanted more than just one picture of the potential dog they were looking at.

High Fidelity Prortyping

For this section of the project we all came together to make sure that the designs were visually cohesive by deciding how components of the site should look across platforms both in style and color. We also had to decide how to layout information because we each had different methods of doing that in the previous prototypes. Adoption Process

The Adoption Process page features a new way of chunking information so that users will be more inclined to read how to get a dog. The information used to be in giant paragraphs but now the subheadings allow readers to find information they are looking for with ease. The mobile is in bullets since the information did not easily fit in full sentences.

Labs Needing Home

This is the page that allows users to see all the available dogs for adoption on the website. We organized the captions so that they would be easy to read including only the dogs name, age and gender. We did this to minimize initial information for each dog so that users would not be overwhelmed with lots of information being presented about each dog when browsing.

Dog Profile Page

The dog profiles allow you to get to know the dogs a little more. Each profile will tell the user whether or not the dogs are good with kids, cats, or other dogs. Each page will also give the users more pictures of the dog as well as allow you to contact the shelter for any questions you have about the specific dog. If you are not looking to adopt the dogs you can always donate to the specific dog on their profile to help them find a happy home.

Final Thoughts and Conclusion

Lessons Learned

When redesigning a companies website it is important to balance user needs and stakeholder preferences when trying to redesign their site. Working on a fast paced assignment makes iterations go fast so user interviews and feedback are key to making improvements in a timely manner.

Do Better

We did not really think about designing a mobile wireframe initially. I wish we would have done that instead of designing the desktop first because it made it really hard for me to shrink features when designing for mobile so that they would fit nicely. We should have started small and then went big instead of doing it the other way around.

Key Takeaways

Understanding the stakeholders goals for the site helps when redesigning because you have a good understanding of what the company is about. Information that is not content chunked becomes overwhelming for users and they most likely will not sit and read big paragraphs. Starting with desktop first makes mobile designs harder since you have to figure out how to fit all the information on a smaller screen without hindering the overall design for a website.
